Thermal Moonquakes


The three major triggers of terrestrial landslides are water, volcanic and tectonic activity, and saturation of water is the most important one. On the Moon, forces that could cause deformation and failure in slope materials include seismic activity induced by impact cratering and endogenic geodynamic processes (e.g., volcanism, tectonism, tides and body shrinkage) and thermal stresses raised by diurnal temperature changes



• The rate of diurnal temperature change is less than 2 K/min on the Moon (Molaro and Byrne, 2012), but grain-scale stresses induced by diurnal temperature changes are on the order of 100 MPa (Molaro et al., 2015). This magnitude of stress is comparable with typical strength of lunar rocks. Thirumalai and Demou (1973) performed physical experiments on lunar analogue samples and found that thermal expansion in vacuum was the same as that in atmosphere, and that the tested samples displayed permanent and irrecoverable strains, which were caused by grain reorientation from the differential expansion. Numerical modelling of thermal cycling on lunar analogue rocks revealed that surface boulders ≤ 30 cm in dimensions exhibit a weak response to thermal forcing, but thermal stresses in boulders larger than 1 m in diameters increase to several tens of MPa, since their surfaces approach that of an infinite halfspace (Molaro et al., 2017). Based on fracture patterns observed in meter-sized boulders, fractures formed by both thermal weathering and micrometeorite impacts were interpreted (Ruesch et al., 2020). It is highly plausible that thermal weathering can break down exposed bedrocks on the Moon, and fast landslides can be triggered by thermal weathering on steep slopes (Molaro et al., 2017).
